#### Foreword
The global situation today is volatile, with the competition between the two major powers, China and the United States, capturing worldwide attention. Some believe that since 2016, underlying intentions for conflict have existed between China and the US, but the two sides have yet to agree on "how to fight." Now, various parts of the world seem to be bracing for potential conflict. But what form might this possible war take, and could it escalate into a global world war?

---

#### I. Background and Motivations of the US-China Competition

##### 1.1 Monetary Expansion and the Perceived Need for War
It's widely known that the United States stimulated its economy through large-scale money printing from 2018 to 2023. Less discussed, however, is that China significantly increased its money supply during the same period, possibly even exceeding the US scale. The US might hope to use a war to "default" on its debt and reshape the global economic order, while China aims to absorb excess production capacity and alleviate domestic inflationary pressures. Although their economic motives differ, both point towards a potential flashpoint: war as a possible means to solve their respective problems.

However, the *form* of war dictates the gains and losses for each side. The US favors limited or proxy wars (like the Russia-Ukraine conflict or the Syrian civil war) to leverage its advantage in existing military equipment stockpiles and extensive combat experience, while avoiding threats to its homeland. China, on the other hand, might be better suited for a total war akin to World War I or II, fully utilizing its vast war potential, endurance, and industrial production capacity. This difference in strategic objectives has led to repeated probing along the brink of conflict, yet without truly "opening fire."

##### 1.2 Probing and Restraint: The Game in the South China Sea and Taiwan Strait
In recent years, the US has frequently made provocative moves in the South China Sea and the Taiwan Strait, deploying carrier strike groups for patrols. Yet, these deployments often involve "arriving and then leaving," a seemingly contradictory action that aligns with its strategic logic. In skirmishes, the US military indeed holds an advantage due to superior equipment and technology, but it dares not escalate actions into a full-blown conflict. This is because China's reaction is exceptionally strong—whenever the US makes a move, China often mobilizes two theater commands to top alert status, deploys its navy en masse, and even points strategic weapons directly ("in your face"). This posture of "fighting to the death" represents the risk of total war that the US desperately seeks to avoid. Consequently, while both sides claim victory in news reports, neither has actually crossed the red line.

##### 1.3 The Shadow of Historical Lessons
The lessons of World War II profoundly influence the decisions of both sides. Japan, swept up by public opinion, expanded its war efforts and ultimately had to attack the US, leading to its total collapse. China has clearly learned this lesson, avoiding rash actions over Taiwan. Attacking Taiwan might intimidate Japan and South Korea in the short term, but it cannot compensate for China's vulnerability in energy resources like oil. Instead, it could trigger a Cold War-style arms race, detrimental to China's long-term interests. Similarly, even if the US were to win a total war against China, the costs would far outweigh the benefits. China lacks valuable natural resources worth plundering, and the West already possesses advantages in technology and human resources. Winning would merely mean "countless casualties for nothing."

#### II. Divergence and Possibility of War Models

##### 2.1 US Preference for Limited War
Since the Cold War, the US has excelled at achieving strategic goals through proxy wars or limited interventions (e.g., Kosovo, Iraq). This model is low-cost, poses controllable risks, and maximizes the advantage of its existing military stockpiles. However, this strategy faces challenges when dealing with China. China is not a small nation; its industrial capacity and mobilization potential far exceed those of previous US adversaries. If a limited conflict escalates, China could rapidly expand the war, forcing the US into the undesired scenario of total war.

Currently, the US's 11 aircraft carriers form the bedrock of its overseas hegemony, but these assets appear vulnerable against China's hypersonic missiles (like the Dongfeng series). If its carriers were damaged, US global deployment capabilities would suffer significantly ("a large part would rot"), which is another reason for its reluctance to escalate easily.

##### 2.2 China's Potential for Total War
China is better suited for protracted and total war. It possesses a complete industrial system, a huge population base, and formidable logistical and mobilization capabilities. War models similar to WWI and WWII would allow China to convert its war potential into actual combat power. However, this model requires immense resource support, especially energy like oil, where China has a significant vulnerability. If the energy problem cannot be swiftly resolved, a protracted war could potentially cripple its economy.

##### 2.3 Assessing the Likelihood of War
Based on this analysis, the probability of a full-scale war between China and the US is relatively low. The delicate balance formed by the US strategy of "limited provocation + strategic withdrawal" and China's "firm response + restrained bottom line" suggests that both sides understand the costs of total war far exceed any potential gains. Even if a localized conflict occurs (such as a Taiwan Strait crisis), it is more likely to conclude as a short, high-intensity encounter rather than evolving into a world war.

#### III. East Asian Geopolitics and Ally Dilemmas

##### 3.1 The Alignment Logic of Japan and South Korea
Japan and South Korea play crucial roles in the US-China competition. Both are economically advanced but highly dependent on Western industrial supply chains and resource imports. When US-China relations ease, they subtly court China ("send flirtatious glances") seeking economic benefits; once tensions escalate, they quickly align with the US. This isn't driven by anti-China sentiment but by survival needs. If China were to gain control over Japan and South Korea, its industrial advantages could squeeze their markets, potentially leading to their economies becoming marginalized ("North Koreanized" or a "return to the Edo period"). Therefore, Japan and South Korea prefer to cling tightly to the US rather than fall into China's sphere of influence.

##### 3.2 Limitations of China's Allies
China's sheer scale makes it difficult to have true allies in the same way the US does. With a population of 400 million and technological advantages, the US can outsource industries to Europe, Japan, South Korea, and even peripheral countries through cooperation. China, with its 1.4 billion population and massive industrial system, is largely self-sufficient but cannot adequately "feed" allies in the same manner. Taking South Korea's shipbuilding industry as an example, losing Western orders would force its ships into direct competition with Chinese products, shrinking its survival space. China's allies tend to be strategic partners (like Pakistan) or resource providers (like Russia), making them naturally "incompatible" with developed economies like Japan and South Korea.

##### 3.3 Decoupling and Countermeasures
The most effective measure the US could take against China is "decoupling"—essentially "handing over" Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan to China's sphere while retreating to Australia and the Second Island Chain. This "let's play separately" strategy could maximally weaken China's influence on global supply chains. However, complete decoupling is unrealistic—cheap industrial goods from East Asia are vital to Western economies. "De-risking" has replaced "decoupling" as the new buzzword. China responds with a strategy of "unyielding principles + flexible Tai Chi," neither fully opposing the US nor allowing the US to completely break ties, maintaining an ambiguous, arms-length relationship.

#### IV. Historical Experience and Strategic Choices

##### 4.1 Japan's Lesson: Restraint and Technology
Japan's over-reliance on territorial and population expansion in WWII, ultimately driven by populism, led it into an abyss. This lesson teaches China that the core of modern warfare is science and technology, not traditional agrarian thinking. China's recent massive investments in technology and the rise of its high-tech industries are a direct response to this lesson.

##### 4.2 The Soviet Union's Lesson: Confrontation and Breakthrough
The Soviet experience offers another perspective: when facing the West, one must project strength ("like a rabbit fighting an eagle") to avoid being slowly weakened ("like a frog in gradually warming water"). If defeat seems inevitable, it's better to escalate dramatically ("smash the pot rather than preserve the cracked shard") than to passively await destruction. The trend towards a more assertive, "North Korean-style" diplomacy from China—characterized by tough statements and a willingness to show force—reflects this strategy.

##### 4.3 US-China Strategic Game
The US attempts to bait China into initiating conflict through strategies of "feigning retreat to capture" (like proposing a G2, or stoking tensions in the Taiwan Strait), aiming to force Western nations to take sides and initiate a new Cold War. However, Chinese leaders remain clear-headed, avoiding Japan's historical mistake. Conversely, China employs the strategy of "befriending distant states while attacking nearby ones" to expand its international space, leveraging the Russia-Ukraine conflict to bind Russia closer, capitalizing on Middle East reconciliation efforts, and gradually breaking through US attempts at isolation.

---

#### V. Future Outlook and Conclusion

##### 5.1 The Possibility of World War
Currently, the competition between China and the US looks more like the prelude to a "Cold War 2.0" than the precursor to a hot war. Full-scale war is a lose-lose proposition for both sides. If the US wins, it gains little of substance; if it loses, its hegemony collapses. If China wins, it struggles to overcome resource constraints; if it loses, its previous progress is wiped out. Compared to total war, decoupling, sanctions, and regional confrontations are more likely to become the norm.

##### 5.2 China's and the US's Strengths and Weaknesses
China, leveraging its industrial advantage and demographic dividend, could likely sustain a hot war for 10-20 years, but a Cold War-style war of attrition is its Achilles' heel. The US is the opposite: its military stockpiles and alliance system provide short-term advantages, but deindustrialization makes enduring prolonged attrition difficult. Both sides are avoiding "flipping the table," preferring to test boundaries within the existing rules.

##### 5.3 Echoes of History
As Mao Zedong stated in *On Protracted War*: "All the enemy's challenges' and 'incitements to action' should be shelved and ignored." China's current strategy appears to be a continuation of this thinking—acting decisively but not rashly. In contrast, the US strategy of "keeping its distance," while rational, also reveals its limitations in effectively containing China.

##### Conclusion
The world might be preparing for *a* war, but this "war" is more likely to be a contest of economics, technology, and geopolitical influence rather than a world war fought with cannons and fire. The competition between China and the US will likely remain tense but manageable for the foreseeable future. Only if one side is pushed into a corner, or if an unpredictable black swan event occurs, might the situation spin completely out of control. For now, the ability to fight enables peace, and restraint ensures long-term survival—perhaps this is the wisdom shared by both sides.
