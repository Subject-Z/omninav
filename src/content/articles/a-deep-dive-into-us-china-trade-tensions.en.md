#### Foreword

Since 2018, the United States and China have been embroiled in trade friction of a scale rarely seen in history. This economic contest, primarily initiated by the U.S., unfolded through measures like tariff hikes and the establishment of trade barriers, capturing widespread global attention. The main justification offered by the U.S. was the long-standing bilateral trade deficit, asserting that America was getting a raw deal. However, are the roots of this dispute truly that simple? This article will delve layer by layer into the surface, deeper, and fundamental causes of the US-China trade friction, striving to present readers with a clear, comprehensive picture.

**Part 1: Beneath the Surface – Is the Trade Deficit the Real Culprit?**

In March 2018, then-U.S. President Trump, citing Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, announced the imposition of tariffs on a vast range of goods imported from China, officially kicking off the trade war. The core U.S. argument was that China's large, persistent trade surplus with the U.S. (America's deficit with China) harmed the U.S. economy and cost American jobs.

**1. What are Trade Surpluses/Deficits and Tariffs?**

* **Trade Surplus/Deficit:** This refers to whether a country's total export value exceeds its total import value (surplus) or falls short of it (deficit) over a specific period. Simply put, a surplus means selling more than buying, and a deficit is the opposite. Data shows that since the mid-1980s, China has indeed maintained a long-term trade surplus with the U.S., resulting in a deficit for the U.S. Before the trade war erupted in 2018, the U.S. merchandise trade deficit with China had already reached several hundred billion dollars annually.
* **Tariff:** This is a tax levied by a country's customs authorities on goods and articles entering or leaving its customs territory. Raising tariffs increases the cost of imported goods, reducing their market competitiveness, with the intent of protecting domestic industries. In a trade war, tariffs are the most direct "weapon." The additional tariffs imposed by the U.S. on Chinese goods were often stacked on top of existing rates, significantly increasing the cost for some Chinese products entering the U.S. market.

**2. Did the Trade Deficit Really Disadvantage the U.S.?**

Looking solely at the trade in goods, it might seem like China was profiting at America's expense. However, economic reality is far more complex.

* **Capital Flowback:** China accumulated vast amounts of U.S. dollar foreign exchange reserves through its trade surpluses. Besides being used for international payments and stabilizing the exchange rate, a significant portion of these reserves was used to purchase U.S. Treasury securities. This means the dollars China earned through trade were effectively lent back to the U.S. government by purchasing its debt. These funds supported U.S. government spending and economic activity, objectively helping maintain lower interest rates in the U.S.
* **Dollar Hegemony and "Seigniorage":** As the world's primary reserve and settlement currency, the U.S. dollar provides its issuer (the United States) with an intangible benefit known as "seigniorage"—the difference between the face value of the currency and its printing cost, which can be used to purchase goods and services globally. Foreign holdings of dollar assets (including Treasuries) also bolster the dollar's international status, allowing the U.S. to continually benefit.

Therefore, simply judging "who wins and who loses" based on the trade deficit is one-sided. In the US-China trade relationship, the U.S. has also derived significant tangible benefits, such as access to low-cost goods and the influx of capital. Its claim of being "disadvantaged" appears more like a pretext for initiating trade friction.

So, what are the deeper reasons beneath the surface?

**Part 2: The Deeper Game – Technological Competition and Global Value Chain Restructuring**

If the trade deficit isn't the core reason, then the deeper motives behind the U.S. initiating the trade war likely point to concerns about China's technological progress and industrial upgrading.

**1. Global Supply Chains and Value Chains**

Modern product manufacturing is incredibly complex, often involving a global division of labor. Take smartphones, for example:
* **Design & Core Technology:** Usually controlled by companies in a few developed countries (like the U.S.), such as chip design and operating systems.
* **Key Components:** Supplied by firms in places like Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan (China), such as memory chips and high-end display screens.
* **General Components & Assembly:** Primarily handled by companies in regions like mainland China.

This network of transnational division of labor and collaboration constitutes the **global supply chain**. The value created at each link in this chain differs, forming the **global value chain (GVC)**. Typically, companies at the top of the value chain (design, branding, core technology) capture the majority of the profits, while those at the bottom (assembly, low-value-added components) earn meager returns. The profit distribution of the iPhone is a classic example: Apple Inc., leveraging its brand and technological advantages, takes most of the profit, while the mainland Chinese companies responsible for assembly receive very little.

**2. Technological Progress and National Competitiveness**

The more companies a nation possesses at the top of the global value chain, the more stable its position in the global economic landscape and the stronger its profitability. These high-profit enterprises can invest heavily in R&D, driving technological advancement in a virtuous cycle. They also contribute significant tax revenue to support national development, including military strength, thereby consolidating the nation's comprehensive power and international influence.

In recent years, China has made significant strides in numerous high-tech fields, particularly spurred by the "Made in China 2025" initiative, leading to rapid development in industries like information technology, new energy vehicles (NEVs), biomedicine, and aerospace. Taking NEVs as an example, China not only leads globally in production, sales, and ownership but has also built formidable competitiveness in key supporting industries like batteries.

This rapid industrial upgrading and technological catch-up has steadily elevated China's position in the global value chain, leading it to compete directly with the U.S. in some high-value-added areas. This is perceived by the U.S. as a serious challenge to its technological hegemony and economic dominance. The fact that the U.S. tariff lists specifically targeted products related to "Made in China 2025" and the precision strikes against Chinese tech giants like Huawei and ZTE corroborate this deeper motive: to curb China's industrial upgrading momentum and maintain its own leading position in the global technological and economic landscape.

**Part 3: The Fundamental Driver – Historical Patterns of International Industrial Transfer**

To understand the fundamental cause of US-China trade friction, we need to broaden our perspective and review the history of global industrial transfer.

**1. The Logic of Industrial Transfer**

Capital always seeks maximum profit. As a country or region develops, costs for factors like labor and land rise, and environmental regulations tighten, diminishing the comparative advantage of certain existing industries (especially labor-intensive and capital-intensive ones). Consequently, these industries tend to relocate to countries or regions with lower costs. This process is **international industrial transfer**. This allows the originating location to focus on higher-value-added industries, while the recipient region gains development opportunities, though potentially facing issues like being locked into low-end production or environmental pressures.

**2. Four Major Global Industrial Transfers**

* **First (Latter half of 19th Century):** UK → USA, Germany, France. Britain transferred early Industrial Revolution industries like textiles and steel, fostering the rise of new industrial powers like the U.S. and Germany.
* **Second (1950s-1980s):** USA → Japan, Western Europe. After WWII, the U.S. shifted traditional industries like steel and textiles abroad, enabling the rapid recovery and development of Japan and Germany. Concurrently, the U.S. upgraded its own industrial structure but also experienced its first trade deficit (in 1971).
* **Third (1980s-Early 21st Century):** USA, Japan, Europe, "Four Asian Tigers" → Mainland China, Southeast Asia, etc. This transfer was massive, involving labor-intensive industries and parts of capital- and technology-intensive sectors (like electronics assembly, some auto manufacturing). China became the "world's factory," deeply integrating into global supply chains and achieving economic takeoff.
* **Fourth (Post-2008 Financial Crisis to Present):** China and other emerging industrial nations → Southeast Asia, South Asia, Africa, etc. (lower-cost regions). As China's own costs rise and its industries upgrade, some low-to-mid-end manufacturing has begun moving outward. The "Belt and Road" initiative has also objectively facilitated this process.

**3. Evolving Roles in US-China Relations**

During the third wave of industrial transfer, the U.S. and Chinese economies developed a highly complementary relationship: the U.S. provided technology, brands, and markets, while China offered low-cost labor and manufacturing capacity. Both sides benefited significantly. American consumers enjoyed inexpensive goods, and U.S. corporations reaped high profits; China gained the capital, technology, and jobs needed for development.

However, as China—leveraging its vast domestic market, sustained R&D investment, and comprehensive industrial system—continually climbed up the value chain, the situation changed. China was no longer content merely being the "world's factory" and began challenging U.S. leadership in fields like 5G, artificial intelligence, electric vehicles, and semiconductors.

At this point, the industrial relationship between the U.S. and China gradually shifted from **complementarity** to **competition**. When the follower begins to encroach upon the leader's core interest areas, the existing harmonious division of labor is disrupted, significantly increasing the likelihood of friction and conflict. The U.S. felt comprehensive competitive pressure from China and attempted, through trade wars, tech wars, and other means, to slow down or even halt China's industrial upgrading, aiming to "lock" China into the middle and lower ends of the value chain to preserve its existing global economic and technological leadership.

**Conclusion**

The US-China trade friction is not an isolated event; it is the result of the combined effects of globalization, the underlying dynamics of capitalist development, international industrial shifts, and changes in the balance of power between nations.

* **On the surface**, the trade deficit served as the U.S. pretext, but this ignores the complex exchange of benefits involving capital flows and the dollar system.
* **At a deeper level**, it is fierce competition over the commanding heights of technology and dominance in future industries, reflecting America's strategic anxiety about China's rise.
* **Fundamentally**, it stems from the historical tide of international industrial transfer. As China evolved from a "student" accepting low-end industries to a "competitor" challenging in high-end sectors, the established international division of labor was impacted, bringing structural contradictions to the fore and sparking this monumental trade dispute.

Understanding these three levels allows for a deeper grasp of the essence of US-China trade friction and its potential profound impact on the future world order. This is not just a contest between two major powers; it also signals that the global economic order is undergoing profound adjustment and reshaping.
